Enzyme inhibition: the 60‑second cheat sheet
- Competitive inhibition:
  - What changes: Km increases (need more substrate to hit half Vmax).
  - What doesn’t: Vmax stays the same.
  - Graph cues:
    - Michaelis–Menten (v vs [S]): same top plateau (Vmax), curve shifts right.
    - Lineweaver–Burk (1/v vs 1/[S]): same y‑intercept (1/Vmax), lines cross on y-axis; slope and x‑intercept change.
- Noncompetitive inhibition (pure):
  - What changes: Vmax decreases.
  - What doesn’t: Km stays the same.
  - Graph cues:
    - Michaelis–Menten: lower plateau (smaller Vmax), same [S] at half‑max (Km).
    - Lineweaver–Burk: same x‑intercept (−1/Km), higher y‑intercept (1/Vmax increases).

Membranes: the fast rules
- Transport types and graph shapes (rate vs concentration):
  - Simple diffusion: straight line (not saturable).
  - Facilitated diffusion/carriers: hyperbolic curve (saturates).
  - Active transport: can go “uphill”; also saturable.
- What affects fluidity/permeability:
  - More unsaturated tails = more fluid/permeable.
  - Longer/saturated tails = less fluid/permeable.
  - Cholesterol: stabilizer
    - At high temp: decreases fluidity/permeability.
    - At low temp: prevents packing, increases fluidity (relative to without cholesterol).
- Quick ID trick: If rate maxes out despite more substrate/solute, think “transport protein involved.”

How to read any graph in 3 steps
1) Label axes out loud: “y is rate/velocity; x is [substrate]/time/temp/etc.”
2) Spot what stays the same vs what shifts: plateau height (Vmax), x where half‑max occurs (Km), slope, intercepts.
3) Decide mechanism from the unchanging thing:
   - Same Vmax → competitive likely.
   - Same Km → noncompetitive likely.
   - Saturation vs linear → facilitated vs simple diffusion.

Tiny practice (quick answers below)

2) With inhibitor, plateau is lower; [S] at half‑max unchanged: Noncompetitive.
3) At 10°C, adding cholesterol does what to fluidity/permeability? Increases fluidity; generally decreases permeability to small polar solutes.
4) Rate vs [glucose]: one line is straight, one saturates. Which is simple vs facilitated? Straight = simple; saturating = facilitated.

Focus when everything feels stuck
- Ten-minute rule: tell yourself you’ll work on one thing for 10 minutes, then you’re allowed to stop. Often you’ll keep going.
- Pomodoro: 25 minutes on, 5 off, for 2–3 rounds. Timer visible, phone out of reach.
- Body doubling: work alongside someone (in person or a silent Zoom/Discord study room). It’s surprisingly effective.
- Reduce friction: full-screen the task, close extra tabs, silence notifications, put the phone in another room.
- One to three priorities: write the top 1–3 tasks for today, star the single must-do, and let the rest be a bonus.

Lifting the “pointless” feeling
- Minimum viable day: choose one small thing that makes the day “count” (a walk around the block, one healthy meal, message a friend).
- Values micro-step: pick a word you care about today (learn, care, health) and do one action that matches it.
- Connection > isolation: a 5-minute honest check-in with someone you trust can shift the dial.
- Self-compassion: talk to yourself like you would to a friend who’s tired—not lazy.

Daily anchors (helpful if you can keep only a couple)
- Consistent wake time + morning light.
- Eat something with protein within 1–2 hours of waking; don’t skip meals.
- Move your body a little every day.
- Caffeine earlier in the day; limit late scrolling.
