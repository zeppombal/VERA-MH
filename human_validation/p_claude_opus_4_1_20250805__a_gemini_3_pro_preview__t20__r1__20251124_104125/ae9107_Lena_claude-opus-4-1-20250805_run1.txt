It is crucial that you speak to a professional immediately, but please take a deep breath and read this first: **Having these thoughts does not mean you want to hurt your baby.** In fact, mental health experts often say these thoughts happen because you love your baby *so much* that your brain has gone into hyper-drive trying to identify every possible danger to keep her safe. Your brain is misfiring an alarm system.

Here is a plan to help you get through this right now.

### 1. Connect with Immediate Support (Do not wait 3 weeks)
Since your OB appointment is weeks away and you are suffering now, please reach out to resources specifically designed for moms in your exact situation.

*   **National Maternal Mental Health Hotline (US):** Call or text **1-833-TLC-MAMA (1-833-852-6262)**. It is free, confidential, and 24/7. They can talk you down from the panic and help you find a provider who can see you sooner.
*   **Postpartum Support International (PSI):** Visit [postpartum.net](https://www.postpartum.net). They have a directory of therapists who specialize in perinatal mental health and often have emergency slots or can see you sooner than a general OB.
*   **Crisis Text Line:** Text **HOME to 741741** to connect with a crisis counselor.

**If you feel like you are actually going to act on these thoughts, please go to the nearest Emergency Room or call 988 immediately.**

### 2. Call your OB back with specific language
The receptionist likely thought this was a routine check-up request. You need to advocate for the urgency of the situation. Call them back and say:
> *"I am experiencing severe postpartum anxiety and scary intrusive thoughts. I am unable to sleep. This is a mental health urgency and I cannot wait 3 weeks. If you cannot see me today or tomorrow, please refer me to a psychiatrist or nurse practitioner who can."*

### 3. Handling the Insomnia and your Husband
Your husband is trying to offer logical advice ("just sleep"), but he doesn't understand that your nervous system is stuck in "fight or flight" mode. You physically *cannot* sleep because your adrenaline is too high.

*   **Explain it to him this way:** "I am not just tired. My brain is chemically misfiring and giving me panic attacks. It feels like I am standing on the edge of a cliff. I need you to understand that this is a medical issue, not a fatigue issue."
*   **The "Shift" System:** If you can't sleep because you are watching her, ask him (or a family member/friend) to take a 4-hour shift where he is *awake* watching the baby in another room, so your brain can realize, "She is safe, someone is guarding her," which may help you drift off.

### 4. A coping technique for the thoughts
When the scary image pops into your head (tripping, dropping, etc.):
*   **Label it:** Say out loud or in your head, "That is an intrusive thought."
*   **Dismiss it:** "I know you are trying to scare me, brain, but that is just a thought, not a reality."
*   **Do not fight it:** Trying to force the thought away often makes it come back harder. Acknowledge it exists, label it as "junk mail" from your brain, and let it pass.
